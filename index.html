Activity: virtual interview

In this activity, I had to record a virtual interview with the help of a classmate. The task was to simulate a job interview for a position related to my professional profile. I first participated as the applicant, and then we switched roles so I also acted as the interviewer.

During the interview, I was asked to answer the following questions:

  • Tell us about yourself.
  • What motivates you?
  • What are your hobbies?
  • What are your future plans if you get this job?
  • What are your future goals or what are your career goals?
  • What do you see as a major success in your life?
  • Why did you leave your last job?
  • Where do you see yourself in 5 years?

This exercise was very useful for practicing work-related vocabulary, improving fluency when answering professional questions, and gaining confidence when expressing myself in English during a job interview.

Vocabulary about Crimes and Punishments

Learning vocabulary about crimes and punishments is very useful, as it not only helps us understand news, movies, and books, but also provides us with the right words to describe situations related to law and justice.

The word "crime" refers to an illegal act, such as theft, murder, or fraud. Punishment, on the other hand, is the penalty a person receives after being convicted, such as paying a fine, going to prison, or performing community service. Both terms are essential when discussing the legal system.

There are many types of crimes, and each has specific vocabulary. For example:

  • Armed robbery: A thief broke into a home at night and stole valuables.
  • Shoplifting: A teenager was caught stealing from a clothing store.
  • Cybercrime: Hackers were arrested for cybercrime after stealing online banking information.
  • Kidnapping: Police rescued a businessman after an attempted kidnapping.
  • Drug trafficking: Several people were arrested for drug trafficking near the border.

These examples show how each word describes a different type of illegal activity.

Vocabulary for punishments is equally important because it illustrates the consequences of breaking the law. Some examples are:

  • Fine: She had to pay a fine for illegal parking.
  • Community service: She performed community service after being convicted of vandalism.
  • Life sentence: The offender was sentenced to life in prison for murder.
  • House arrest: The young offender remained under house arrest for six months.

These expressions show the range of penalties, from light to very serious.

In legal situations, we often find this vocabulary in questions and commands used by criminals and police officers. For example:

  • A criminal might ask, "May I call my lawyer?" or "What are my rights?"
  • A police officer might say, "You are under arrest" or "Please get out of the car."

Vocabulary related to crimes can also be used in different grammatical structures, such as direct and indirect speech:

Direct speech: ‚ÄúA thief stole my purse.‚Äù

Indirect speech: ‚ÄúShe said a thief had stolen her purse.‚Äù

This demonstrates that learning the words isn't enough; we also need to practice using them in context.

In conclusion, studying vocabulary about crimes and punishments expands our English skills by providing us with precise words to describe illegal actions, legal processes, and justice. It connects language learning to real-life situations, making our communication more precise and effective.

Critical Thinking and Media Literacy

Mass media surrounds us daily through television, newspapers, films, the internet, advertisements, and more. Each form of media communicates a message, whether explicit or hidden, and these messages influence how we see the world. To fully understand media texts, critical thinking is essential. Critical thinking involves analyzing information beyond its surface, questioning hidden intentions, and evaluating arguments to arrive at informed conclusions.

Media literacy extends critical thinking by teaching us to ‚Äúread‚Äù media texts more deeply. This means taking a second look at what we see or hear to uncover underlying meanings. Media often simulates reality through special effects, editing, or persuasive techniques, making fictional or manipulated content appear real. Developing media literacy allows individuals to recognize these strategies and avoid being misled or manipulated by them.

Media messages are never neutral. Each text has a purpose whether to inform, entertain, persuade, or profit and is designed for a specific audience defined by factors such as age, gender, lifestyle, and values. Advertisers, for example, use strategies like identification, generalization, emotional appeal, and celebrity endorsement to connect with audiences and influence their choices. Similarly, news or documentaries may claim objectivity, but every story is shaped by the perspective of its creator.

Ultimately, interpreting media depends on the audience. Media texts express values, ideologies, and intentions, and viewers must actively question and evaluate these messages. By asking who created the text, what techniques are used, what ideologies are represented, and what the author‚Äôs objectives are, individuals can form their own conclusions. Media literacy empowers people to interpret messages critically, rather than simply accepting what they are told to believe.
